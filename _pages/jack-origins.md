        <h1 class="mb-4">The Origins of the Name Jack</h1>
        <p>
          The personal name <strong>Jack</strong> ultimately derives from the Hebrew name 
          <em>Yaʿăqōḇ</em> (יַעֲקֹב), meaning "he grasps the heel" or "he supplants," 
          referencing the biblical narrative of Jacob grasping Esau's heel at birth.
        </p>
        <h3 class="mt-4">Linguistic Journey</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Hebrew (c. 1200–500 BCE)</strong> – <em>Yaʿăqōḇ</em>, preserves /j/ ("y" in yes)</li>
          <li><strong>Greek (3rd–2nd c. BCE)</strong> – Ἰάκωβος (<em>Iákōbos</em>), transliteration preserves /j/ sound</li>
          <li><strong>Latin (4th–5th c. CE)</strong> – Iacobus / Jacobus, initial /j/ preserved</li>
          <li><strong>Old French / Anglo-Norman (10th–13th c. CE)</strong> – Jacques / Jacce, palatalization begins (/dʒ/)</li>
          <li><strong>English (13th–14th c. CE)</strong> – Jacce → Jack, vernacular form stabilized, /dʒ/ sound</li>
        </ul>
        <h3 class="mt-4">Phonetic Notes</h3>
        <p>
          The initial <em>Ya</em> in Hebrew represents the palatal approximant <strong>/j/</strong> — the "y" in <em>yes</em>. 
          This sound is preserved through Greek and Latin transliterations and palatalized to <strong>/dʒ/</strong> 
          in Old French and English, giving rise to the modern pronunciation of <strong>Jack</strong>.
        </p>
        <h3 class="mt-4">Summary</h3>
        <p>
          The evolution of Jack illustrates how a name can traverse languages, scripts, and cultures, 
          undergoing phonetic and orthographic changes while retaining its original semantic core.
        </p>
